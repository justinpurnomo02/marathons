Background

Carlsbad has been on my radar for a while, as I used to live in Oceanside, a neighboring city, back in middle school. The area holds a lot of nostalgia for me, and I was excited to race on familiar roads.

Additionally, Kris Hui - a running influencer I follow - ran Carlsbad as her first marathon, and her subsequent vlog sold me on the course. I decided to sign up for it after passing my PhD qualifying exam, as some twisted form of celebration. What better way to recover from one grueling experience than to sign up for another?

Having ran a 3:31 in my previous marathon, I began my Carlsbad training block with a 3:20 time goal. However, my long runs went so well that I decided to go for 3:10 five weeks before race day.

Pre-race

At the Carlsbad expo!

I arrived at the Carlsbad Expo Saturday afternoon to pick up my race bib, and I got to meet two people who were a part of the pacing crew. When I told them that I was planning to be in the 3:10 pace group, they assured me that I was in good hands. Apparently, the 3:10 pacer (a 62 year old man named Bin) has ran Carlsbad for years and knew the course well.

A couple hours later, I had the opportunity to grab Chipotle with an old friend who would be running in the half. What really impressed me was that he completed a 50K just the week before. I don‚Äôt think I‚Äôll be participating in any official ultramarathons anytime soon, but I would definitely like to run a 50K sometime by myself. After dinner with my friend, I drove with my sister to grab a hefty baguette from Panera so I could carb load.

I woke up at 3AM the morning of the race to shower, change, and fuel up. We arrived at the race site an hour before the race, so I was able to warm up and use the bathroom two times before the 6:15AM start time. I was surprised by how many porta-potties were available; normally there's never-ending lines of people waiting to use them. At 6:05AM, I lined up at the start line, positioning myself a couple feet away from Bin, the 3:10 pacer.

Mile 1-13

Since this race is relatively small, the start was relatively low energy. I had some difficulty starting my Strava when we started so I was fiddling with my watch for the first 0.1 miles. Even though it eventually worked, I was a bit annoyed that my watch would be inaccurate for the entirety of the race, and that the final recorded distance would likely be less than 26.2 miles.

I tucked myself in a pretty sizable group of people who also wanted to run 3:10, and began clipping miles with ease. A part of me wondered if I should have perhaps went with the 3 hour group, but thankfully I did not let my emotions get the better of me. I told myself numerous times during training that I had to resist the temptation to deviate from the 3:10 plan. Early race miles are always free since you're running off of pure adrenaline. The fast miles will catch up to you eventually if you aren't careful.

Miles 14-20

This is probably what happened to many runners today, as the 3:10 pace group began to thin out at this point. I think there were around six of us still remaining in the 3:10 pace group. I committed to staying steady until mile 20, which was when I would assess whether or not I could make a push.

Miles 21-24

What was left of the 3:10 pace group completely fell apart here. Half of the group surged ahead, while the other half began to lag behind. I decided to bet on myself and surge ahead with this middle aged man wearing a purple sleeveless shirt. I later found out after the race he was able to get a Boston qualifying time of 3:08. I think he needed a 3:10 for his age, but I could be mistaken. I stuck right beside him for a little while, and he complimented me for keeping up. I also saw my middle school friend on the other side of the road, since the half started an hour and a half after the full and the courses overlapped with each other.

I soon began to feel slight pulses in my calves, so I decided to let purple shirt guy go off ahead. A spectator must have seen me struggling since she saw my bib and cheered for me by name. I wish I could have said something back, but I was so terrified my calf would just lock up I didn't want to waste any breath. I made eye contact with a volunteer who was handing out water and I stretched out my arm. He pointed at me and yelled something along the lines of "I got you!!"

Miles 25-26.2

Bin caught back up to me at this point, so I decided that I was going to try to latch onto him for the rest of the race. Bin must have had some unexpected pain or something though, as he began to slow down during mile 25. He urged me to keep going, which slightly scared me since that meant I would be running in no man's land. I ended up running with some half marathoners, but it wasn't quite the same.

Bin ended up catching back up to (and passing) me as we got to mile 26, and unfortunately I was unable to stick with him. I felt like I had so much more to give, but I knew my calves would lock up if I pushed the pace by even just a hair. I ended up limping across the finish line in 3:10:52. I made sure to thank Bin afterwards- I don't think I could've done it without his steady pacing and encouragement.

As a side note, I was wise to not push any harder. Once I met my mom and sister at the finishers' area, my left calf and right thigh cramped so bad I almost cried. Medical staff checked in with me to make sure I was okay. Sometimes I don't know why I willingly sign up for these races...

Takeaways

Increasing mileage to 60-70 miles per week during this training block paid dividends. I had so much endurance, and as I said earlier, I felt like I had so much more to give. But even the strongest aerobic base can only get you so far if you lack the strength to maintain a fast speed. I think that's the biggest takeaway for my third marathon. I'm a pretty weak runner and I hope to change that in the next time.

One thing I want to highlight is how seamless Carlsbad was. The event was so well-organized, clean, and contained. My favorite marathon as of yet, and I could definitely see myself coming back again for a future race.

I think I've gotten to a point where many of my non-runner friends would consider me relatively fast. Of course I recognize there is still a lot of room for improvement, but most people remember 6-7 minutes being their fastest mile time, so I suppose it's saying something if I can average 7:17 min per mile, 26.2 times. It feels good to be a 3:10:XX marathoner, but I know that there's still so much I can do to get even faster. Needless to say, I'll be gunning for sub 3 next time.

The first couple miles were pure chaos and congestion. I felt encaged early on, but I resisted the temptation to weave in and out, focusing on conserving energy. Miles 8-17

With the half-marathoners gone, it was obvious who was in the 3:30 pack. The pack comprised of women who I assumed were trying to qualify for Boston, and then these massive shirtless men. I felt like the odd man out, but it was inspiring to be running with a diverse group of people who all shared the same goal. The pacer made sure to give us advice all throughout the course, telling us where to expect water, uphills, etc. 10/10 pacer would recommend. At mile 13, I texted my sister that I felt amazing. Famous last words...

Miles 18-22

I think it was around mile 18 that I felt my right calf beginning to pulse, which gave me this huge feeling of dread. I tried to ignore it, but the thought of an impending cramp kept gnawing at me. I needed to fuel up, but I only had one more gel. I was also really thirsty, so I took advantage of every water station I saw from that point on. I also noticed that the pulsing in my right calf worsened whenever I landed on an uneven surface, so I made sure to keep my eyes on the ground to make sure I was only running on smooth surfaces.

Miles 23-26.2

There was an incline at mile 23 that the pacer warned us about, so I wanted to stop by the water station right before it. In order to drink all the water from the cup (and to wait for the 3:30 group to catch up to me), I slowed down to walking pace at the mile 23 water station. . . which was a huge mistake. The adrenaline from running wore off, and the cramp hit me hard. I was able to still run, but I saw myself getting left behind by the 3:30 group.

At this point it was all about damage control. I started doing mental calculations to see if I could at least attain my initial 3:40 goal. The pain was so unbearable I began walking... but this woman who was also left behind by the 3:30 group caught up to me and encouraged me to keep running. From then on, I alternated between running and walking. I alternated between walking 20 steps and running as long as I could before the cramps returned. It was so brutal, and all I could think about was how I needed to pay more respect to the marathon distance. I‚Äôm pretty bad at hiding my physical pain, so I know for a fact that all the spectators saw me writhing in agony.‚Äù I remember one spectator in the last half mile shouting, "Finish the race!!" Takeaways

I‚Äôm realizing I only focused on the negatives, so I should make it a point that I was so happy and thankful to be a part of this marathon. It was so cool running alongside thousands of runners, and the whole experience reminded me why I even run in the first place. I improved so much from the 2023 Orange County Marathon too. A 50 minute PR is quite impressive if I do say so myself.

That said, there‚Äôs still plenty of room for improvement: higher weekly mileage, more long runs, better nutrition, and optimized running gear. Last training cycle I averaged 27 miles per week; this time, 45. Next time, I‚Äôd like to hit the 60-mile range. 2030 Boston Marathon perhaps?
